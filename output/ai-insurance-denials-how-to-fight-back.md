## The algorithm in the room: how AI is making your coverage decisions

Artificial intelligence in insurance coverage decisions isn't a future concern. It's the present reality, operating largely out of sight, behind the form letters and hold music most patients know too well. Understanding what you're actually up against is the first step toward beating it.

---

### What is utilization review AI and why are insurers using it?

Utilization review is the process insurers use to determine whether a requested treatment, procedure, or medication is "medically necessary" and therefore covered under your plan. Historically, this meant a human clinician, often a nurse or physician hired by the insurer, reviewing your medical records and applying coverage criteria to your specific case. That model still exists on paper. In practice, it's been largely replaced by algorithmic insurance decisions.

Insurers adopted utilization review AI for straightforward business reasons: speed, scale, and cost reduction. Processing hundreds of millions of prior authorization requests annually with human reviewers is expensive and slow. Automated systems can process requests in seconds, flagging only a subset for human escalation. UnitedHealth Group's nH Predict tool (developed through its NaviHealth subsidiary) and Cigna's automated review system have both been documented in investigative reporting and litigation as systems that generate denial recommendations with minimal individualized physician input. 

---

### The scale of the problem — how many claims are AI-denied?

The numbers are striking. The American Medical Association reports that prior authorization requests have surged, physicians in one AMA survey completed an average of 45 prior authorization requests per physician per week, with 94% reporting that the process delays patient care. The federal government processed over 35 million Medicare Advantage prior authorization requests in a single year, and investigations found that a significant share of denials were later reversed on appeal, suggesting they were wrongful in the first place. 

At that volume, automated claims processing isn't a convenience, it's the only operational model that scales. But scale is precisely the problem. The sheer volume makes individualized human medical review virtually impossible, which is not just an ethical failure but, as the legal section below covers, a significant legal one. A 2023 Senate investigation found that several major insurers had denial rates far exceeding industry averages, with algorithmic tools flagging claims for denial based on pattern-matching rather than patient-specific clinical review. 

---

### What AI gets wrong — the population data problem

AI denial systems are trained on population-level data: statistical patterns derived from thousands or millions of historical claims. The algorithm learns what treatments were approved or denied for certain diagnosis codes, age ranges, and demographic profiles. It then applies those population-level patterns to your individual request.

The fundamental problem is that you are not a population. A 67-year-old woman with Type 2 diabetes, a recent cardiac event, and early-stage kidney disease is not a data point. She's a person with a unique clinical picture that may make a treatment medically necessary for her even if it's statistically denied for her demographic group. This creates a concrete legal vulnerability: because the AI cannot demonstrate that its population-based criteria actually applied to your specific case, a well-constructed, individualized medical argument has real leverage at every stage of the appeals process.

## Red flags — how to tell if AI denied your claim

Most patients receive a denial and assume a physician somewhere reviewed their file and made a professional judgment. The signs below tell a different story.

---

### The speed tell — when your denial arrives too fast

If your prior authorization request was denied within minutes, or in some documented cases, within seconds, that timeline is physically incompatible with meaningful physician review. A clinician cannot read a medical record, assess clinical guidelines, consider your history, and render a professional judgment in the time it takes to microwave a cup of coffee. A near-instant denial almost certainly came from an algorithm.

ProPublica and STAT News both published investigations documenting Cigna's automated review process, in which the company's system allegedly allowed medical reviewers to deny claims at a rate of dozens per hour, averaging seconds per case in some accounts. Check the timestamp on your denial documentation against the time you submitted your request. If the gap is suspiciously short, document it. That timestamp becomes evidence in your appeal.

---

### Templated language and vague medical justifications

Read your denial letter carefully, then read it again. Boilerplate language, phrases like "not medically necessary based on applicable clinical guidelines" with no specific guideline cited, no reference to your actual medical records, and no explanation of how the criteria apply to your case, is a signature of algorithmic processing. If the denial letter could have been sent to any of a thousand different patients without changing a word, that's a red flag.

Federal law is on your side here. Both ERISA and the ACA require insurers to provide specific reasons for denials, including the specific plan provisions or clinical standards they relied on. Vague, templated denial language may itself be a compliance violation, worth stating explicitly when you file your appeal. If you spot identical or near-identical language on denial letters for completely unrelated claim types, that's further evidence of automated processing rather than individualized review.

---

### How to request your denial documentation

Your first concrete action step, before you write a single word of an appeal, is to demand the full denial documentation. Under ERISA and ACA rules, you're entitled to the specific clinical criteria used to evaluate your claim, the name and credentials of any clinician who reviewed your case, the exact guideline or coverage policy invoked, and a copy of your full claim file. Health insurance transparency is a legal requirement, not a courtesy.

Send your request in writing, email with read receipt, or certified mail, and keep copies of everything. Your request can be straightforward:

> *"Pursuant to ERISA Section 503 and applicable ACA regulations, I am requesting the complete documentation supporting the denial of my claim dated [DATE], including: the specific clinical criteria applied, the identity and credentials of all reviewing clinicians, the exact coverage policy or clinical guideline cited, and a complete copy of my claim file."*

What you receive back, or what they fail to provide, will shape your appeal strategy.

## Know your rights — the legal shield you didn't know you had

Federal and state law have built a framework of patient protections that, when understood and invoked correctly, give you real leverage against even the largest insurance companies.

---

### ERISA protections for employer-sponsored plans

If your health insurance comes through your employer, it's almost certainly governed by the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA). ERISA gives you the right to a full and fair review of any denied claim, a written explanation citing specific plan language, and the right to appeal through the plan's internal process before pursuing external remedies. When your health insurance claim is denied, ERISA is your first line of legal defense.

One critical limitation: ERISA typically preempts state tort law claims, meaning you generally cannot sue your insurer for damages like pain and suffering under state law if you have an employer plan. This makes navigating the federal appeals process correctly, and preserving your right to external review, essential. The upside is that ERISA allows recovery of attorney's fees in successful litigation, which makes attorney involvement financially viable in high-value denial cases.

---

### ACA rights and state insurance law protections

For plans purchased on the individual or small-group market, the Affordable Care Act provides strong protections, including the right to an external independent review, covered in detail in the appeals roadmap below. The ACA also requires non-grandfathered plans to cover essential health benefits and prohibits denials based on pre-existing conditions, creating additional grounds to challenge certain rejections.

Patient rights when insurance uses AI to deny claims are also increasingly being written into state law. California, New York, and Illinois are among the states that have passed or are actively advancing legislation requiring greater transparency in algorithmic coverage decisions, mandating human review of AI-generated denials, or imposing new disclosure requirements on insurers. Find your state insurance commissioner at the NAIC's consumer portal, they're both a resource and an escalation path if your appeal fails.

---

### Recent regulatory and legislative developments

The regulatory picture is shifting, though slowly. CMS finalized new prior authorization rules in 2024 requiring Medicare Advantage plans and certain other payers to accelerate response times and improve denial transparency. Multiple federal bills targeting algorithmic insurance denials have been introduced in Congress, and the Department of Labor has signaled increased scrutiny of ERISA plan compliance. High-profile lawsuits against UnitedHealth Group and Cigna, specifically targeting AI-driven denials, have generated both legal pressure and extensive public documentation of how these systems operate.

Regulatory change is coming, but it hasn't fully arrived. For now, individual patient advocacy is the most effective tool available, and the legal framework to support it already exists.

## Building your case — documentation strategies that win appeals

A strong appeal is built on evidence, not emotion. This section covers the specific documentation strategy that separates winning appeals from ones that fail.

---

### The evidence stack — what to gather before you appeal

Before you write a single sentence of your appeal, assemble your complete evidence package. Here's your prioritized checklist:

- ✅ **Original denial letter**, with timestamps preserved
- ✅ **Full Explanation of Benefits (EOB)**, showing exactly how the claim was processed
- ✅ **Insurer's coverage criteria**, the specific clinical policy or guideline cited (request this if not provided)
- ✅ **Complete medical records** supporting medical necessity, from all relevant providers
- ✅ **Letter of Medical Necessity (LMN)**, from your treating physician, addressing the specific denial criteria
- ✅ **Peer-reviewed clinical literature**, studies supporting the effectiveness of the denied treatment
- ✅ **Documentation of the request timeline**, timestamps showing the speed of denial
- ✅ **Names and credentials of any reviewing clinicians**, or documentation that none were provided

The Letter of Medical Necessity deserves special attention. It's your single most powerful document because it does exactly what the AI cannot: it addresses your specific clinical situation through your physician's professional judgment. A strong LMN directly counters the population-level logic of the algorithmic denial with individualized clinical testimony.

---

### How to write a winning appeal letter

Your appeal letter should be precise, assertive, and evidence-anchored, not emotional. Open by identifying the denial (date, claim number, specific service denied) and stating the legal basis for your appeal: *"Pursuant to ERISA Section 503 and the plan's internal appeal procedures..."* or *"Pursuant to ACA external review rights..."* This signals immediately that you know your rights.

Then work through the denial systematically. For each reason cited, respond with specific counter-evidence from your documentation. A direct, plainly worded argument is more effective than a legalistic one: *"The denial appears to have been generated using population-level algorithmic criteria that are not capable of accounting for this patient's individual clinical circumstances, as documented in the attached Letter of Medical Necessity and supporting medical records."* This puts the insurer on notice that you understand what happened.

Close with a clear demand: overturn the denial, provide a specific clinical justification for upholding it, and confirm the next steps in the appeals process. Send everything via certified mail and keep a complete copy.

---

### Involving your doctor and getting external support

A physician who actively participates in your appeal dramatically improves your odds. Peer-to-peer review, a phone call between your treating physician and the insurer's medical director, is one of the most effective single interventions available, and studies suggest it results in denial reversals at significant rates. Ask your doctor to request this call proactively; many insurers grant it as a matter of standard procedure when asked.

For complex or high-value cases, consider bringing in external support. Nonprofit patient advocacy organizations, including the Patient Advocate Foundation, offer free case management services. Professional patient advocates can navigate the appeals process on your behalf. And for denials involving tens of thousands of dollars or more, consulting an attorney who specializes in ERISA or insurance law is worth considering, particularly because attorney's fees can be recovered in successful ERISA litigation.

## The appeals roadmap — step-by-step from denial to overturn

The appeals process has a defined structure. Understanding the stages, and what's at stake at each one, is essential to navigating it effectively.

---

### Stage 1 — internal appeal (the first battleground)

Your internal appeal is filed directly with your insurer, typically within 180 days of receiving your denial (though some plans have shorter windows, so check immediately). Submit everything in writing, via certified mail with return receipt, and keep a complete copy of everything you send. Include your complete evidence stack, your appeal letter, and any supporting documentation from your physician.

Internal appeals are often underestimated. A significant percentage of wrongful denials get reversed at this stage, because submitting individualized medical evidence forces an actual human reviewer to engage with your specific case for the first time. A 2022 KFF analysis found that insurers overturned a substantial share of denied claims on internal appeal when patients actually filed one, yet the vast majority of patients never appeal at all. Mishandling or bypassing this stage can also jeopardize your right to external review, so don't skip it.

---

### Stage 2 — external independent review (your strongest tool)

If your internal appeal is denied, you have, for most ACA-compliant plans, the right to external independent review. An independent review organization (IRO), not affiliated with or paid by your insurer, reviews the denial and renders a decision the insurer is typically required to accept.

External reviewers are not bound by the insurer's internal guidelines and are specifically tasked with evaluating medical necessity on clinical grounds. This is where the AI's population-level logic is most legally exposed: an independent physician reviewer evaluates whether the treatment was medically necessary for this patient, not whether it fits a statistical pattern. Success rates in external review are meaningfully higher than most patients expect, particularly when the appeal is well-documented. Request external review immediately upon receiving your final internal denial, deadlines are strict.

---

### Stage 3 — escalation options when appeals fail

If both internal and external appeals are exhausted without resolution, you still have options. File a formal complaint with your state insurance commissioner, this triggers regulatory scrutiny and often prompts a response from the insurer. Contact your state's Attorney General's office if you believe you've been subjected to bad-faith denial practices. Medicare and Medicaid enrollees can file complaints directly with CMS.

For employer-sponsored plans under ERISA, litigation in federal court remains an option, and given the attorney's fee provisions, it's one that insurers take seriously. As a last resort, media escalation, particularly in cases involving urgent medical need, has proven effective, since major insurers are highly sensitive to reputational risk right now. Persistence itself is a strategy: many insurers reverse denials when faced with continued, documented, escalating pressure.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can I find out if AI was used to deny my claim?**
A: You can request your full claim file and the credentials of any reviewing clinician under ERISA and ACA rules. If no physician is named, or if the denial timeline is implausibly short, those are strong indicators of algorithmic involvement. Some states now require insurers to disclose AI use in coverage decisions, check your state insurance commissioner's website for current rules.

**Q: How long do I have to file an appeal?**
A: For most employer plans under ERISA, you have 180 days from the date of denial to file an internal appeal. ACA plans have similar timelines, but your specific plan documents govern, check your Summary Plan Description immediately. Missing deadlines can forfeit your appeal rights.

**Q: What if I can't afford an attorney?**
A: Many patient advocacy organizations offer free assistance, including the Patient Advocate Foundation and state-level legal aid organizations. AI tools like Fight Health Insurance can help you build a strong appeal at no cost. Attorney involvement, when warranted, is often on a contingency basis for high-value ERISA cases.

**Q: Does a peer-to-peer review actually work?**
A: Yes, peer-to-peer review, where your physician speaks directly with the insurer's medical director, is one of the highest-leverage interventions available. Studies and physician surveys consistently show that peer-to-peer reviews result in denial reversals at significant rates. Ask your doctor to request one as soon as you receive a denial.

**Q: What if my employer-sponsored plan denies my external review right?**
A: ERISA-governed plans are subject to the Department of Labor's claims procedure regulations, which include external review rights for most plans. If your plan disputes this, contact the Department of Labor's Employee Benefits Security Administration (EBSA) at or consult an ERISA attorney.
