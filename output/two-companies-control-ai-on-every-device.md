# The AI Monopoly Risk No One Is Talking About: Two Companies Now Control the AI Layer of Almost Every Device You Own

This isn't an argument against Google or Microsoft. 
It's an argument for noticing what just happened — 
and asking whether anyone should have that much 
control over how the world gets answers.

Think about the last time you asked your phone a question, let your laptop autocomplete a document, or got a suggested answer inside your email app. Chances are, the AI behind that moment came from one of exactly two companies. Not six. Not twelve. Two. The consolidation most analysts describe as a looming future concern has, quietly and without much public debate, already arrived. Google and Microsoft together control the default AI layer on virtually every major consumer and enterprise device on the planet, from Android phones to iPhones, from Windows desktops to the productivity apps a billion office workers open every morning.

Most people have no idea this happened, and they have no real way to opt out of it. By the time you finish reading, you'll understand how this consolidation occurred, why default settings are the most consequential battlefield in modern tech, what it means for your data and your choices, and what questions should be put to these companies before this hardens into permanent infrastructure.

## How two companies came to own the default AI layer on almost every device

The strange thing about this consolidation is that there was no single moment it crystallized. There was no merger filing that dominated the news cycle, no Senate floor drama, no obvious turning point. It happened the way most tectonic shifts in tech happen, incrementally, announced as partnerships, packaged as product improvements, and absorbed by the public as just another update notification.

### The Google Gemini expansion — from Android to iPhone

Google Gemini began its march to ubiquity where Google always starts: Android. As the operating system powering roughly 72% of smartphones globally, Android gave Google a ready-made distribution channel for Gemini that no competitor could replicate overnight. Gemini became the default AI assistant across Android devices worldwide, replacing Google Assistant in a rollout that felt like a software upgrade rather than a market consolidation event.

Then came the pivot that generated far fewer headlines than it deserved. Google is reportedly in an agreement to power core components of Apple Intelligence, the AI layer Apple introduced with iOS 18, effectively making Google the AI engine behind features on iPhones as well. This mirrors the existing Google Search default deal with Apple, which the DOJ estimates is worth between $15 billion and $20 billion annually and is already the subject of a landmark antitrust case. 

The arithmetic is stark. iOS and Android together account for more than 99% of the global smartphone market. If Google's AI infrastructure is running on both platforms, even partially, then the overwhelming majority of smartphone users in the world are, knowingly or not, inside Google's AI inference pipeline. "Google Gemini on Apple iPhone" is no longer a hypothetical competitive scenario. It describes the current default reality.

### The Microsoft–OpenAI axis — owning the desk, the cloud, and the enterprise

While Google secured the mobile layer, Microsoft moved with equal precision on the enterprise and productivity side. Microsoft's multibillion-dollar investment in OpenAI, reported at over $13 billion across multiple funding rounds, translated almost immediately into Microsoft Copilot being embedded natively across the entire Microsoft product ecosystem. 

That ecosystem is not small. Microsoft 365, formerly Office, is used by more than 1 billion people worldwide. Windows 11 ships with Copilot integrated directly into the taskbar. GitHub Copilot has become the dominant AI coding assistant in professional software development. Azure provides the cloud infrastructure for a significant portion of enterprise AI deployments across industries. The Microsoft–OpenAI story is not about one product. It's about one company owning the productivity and enterprise infrastructure that most professionals touch before they even check their phones in the morning.

Together, the picture looks like this: Google owns the mobile and consumer surface. Microsoft owns the enterprise and productivity surface. Between them, they cover nearly every digital environment most people inhabit during a working day. This is AI market concentration with no real precedent in the speed with which it formed.

### Why this happened without a public debate

There are structural reasons this consolidation occurred without triggering the scrutiny it deserved. AI integration was consistently framed as a product feature, Gemini wasn't a market takeover, it was "a smarter assistant." Copilot wasn't a lock-in strategy, it was "AI built right into the tools you already use." The language of product marketing successfully displaced the language of market structure.

The deals were announced as partnerships, cooperative, even exciting, rather than dominance plays that foreclosed competition. Regulators were also genuinely behind the curve; by the time congressional committees began asking serious questions about large language models, the default placements were already in place, the data flywheels were already spinning, and the window for structural intervention had meaningfully narrowed. The story is not that consolidation might happen. It already has.

## The default trap — why factory settings are the real competitive battlefield

Default placement is not a minor UX detail. It's arguably the most consequential competitive position in all of technology, and understanding this changes how you read every AI deal announced from this point forward.

### What behavioral science tells us about defaults

The default effect is one of the most robust findings in behavioral economics: people overwhelmingly stick with whatever option is presented to them as the standard choice, even when alternatives are freely available and switching costs are low. In technology specifically, research consistently shows that more than 90% of users never change default settings on their smartphones or browsers. 

This has a direct implication for AI. If you never opted into Google's AI powering your iPhone assistant, you also can't meaningfully opt out, because most users don't know there's anything to opt out of, let alone where to find the setting that would let them. Default AI on smartphones is not the starting point of a choice. For the overwhelming majority of users, it is the choice. The alternative never enters the picture.

### The browser and search wars were a preview — and we didn't learn the lesson

We've been here before. Google's default search deal with Apple is already the centerpiece of a DOJ antitrust case that the presiding judge has called one of the most significant antitrust actions in decades. The core allegation: Google paid to be the default search engine on iPhones, which locked up distribution so thoroughly that no search competitor could achieve the query volume needed to build a competitive product.

The AI default deal is the direct commercial successor to that arrangement, and regulators are, once again, arriving after the market position has solidified. The browser wars of the 2000s, the search wars of the 2010s, and the AI default layer of the 2020s all follow the same pattern: distribution locks in users, user volume generates data, data compounds advantage, and by the time anyone formally intervenes, restructuring the market requires breaking something that billions of people have come to treat as infrastructure. AI antitrust regulation is chasing a train that has already left the station.

### When "you can download an alternative" isn't a real answer

The standard industry rebuttal to monopoly concerns is always some version of: "But competition exists! Users can download Claude, or Perplexity, or Mistral." This is technically true and practically misleading. The issue is not whether alternatives exist, it's whether they can compete on structurally equal terms. They can't.

Default access confers three compounding advantages that downloaded alternatives can't replicate at volume: first-mover data collection at scale, continuous behavioral feedback loops that improve model performance, and the trust that comes from being the option the device manufacturer endorsed. A user who could switch but doesn't is, from a market structure standpoint, a captured user, not a freely choosing one. The difference between nominal competition and effective competition is the difference between a market that looks diverse and one that actually is.

## This isn't just about antitrust — it's about your data, your choices, and who shapes what you know

The market share numbers matter, but they're not the most important part of this story for most readers. What matters more is what AI concentration at the device layer actually does to everyday digital life.

### The data flywheel problem — concentration compounds itself

AI models get smarter through data, specifically, through enormous volumes of real-world behavioral data showing how humans actually phrase questions, what answers they find satisfying, and what contexts surround their queries. Default placement on billions of devices means that Google and Microsoft collect this behavioral data at a scale that any independent AI company structurally cannot match. This creates what researchers call a data flywheel: better data produces better models, better models win more default deals, more default deals generate more data, and the cycle continues.

Every query you make to your default phone assistant, every autocomplete suggestion you accept in a Microsoft 365 document, every AI-synthesized answer your device serves you, all of that behavioral signal flows back into systems owned by one of two companies. Users have no meaningful say in being the fuel, no transparency about how their interactions train future models, and no share of the value their data creates. Big tech AI control is not an abstract concern. It describes the terms of your daily digital life.

### The epistemic risk — two companies shaping how billions of people get answers

The concern that gets the least attention in mainstream coverage may deserve the most. When two AI systems answer the majority of the world's questions, the biases, blind spots, and commercial incentives of those two systems become the cognitive infrastructure of everyday life.

This is a structural observation, not a conspiracy claim. Consider the domains where people increasingly turn to AI for synthesized answers: health symptoms and treatment options, legal rights and procedures, financial decisions, news summarization, political context. The AI systems answering those questions are not neutral conduits, they reflect training choices, content moderation decisions, partnerships with publishers and advertisers, and the legal risk tolerances of publicly traded corporations. When two companies effectively own that output layer for billions of people, their particular versions of accuracy, balance, and relevance become the default epistemic environment. That's an enormous amount of influence concentrated in an unusually small number of decision-makers.

## Antitrust in the age of AI — why existing frameworks are struggling to keep up

Regulation is not absent from this story. But it's arriving late, and its tools were not designed for the specific shape of this problem.

### What existing antitrust law was built to catch — and what it wasn't

The dominant antitrust framework in the United States is the consumer welfare standard, which focuses primarily on whether market behavior harms consumers through higher prices or reduced output. That framework struggles badly with AI platforms. The products are zero-price or bundled into existing subscriptions, and the harm is not a price increase, it's competitive suppression, data consolidation, and the foreclosure of alternatives. Those harms are real but don't map cleanly onto the doctrinal tools regulators have been using for forty years.

The EU's Digital Markets Act is further ahead on this terrain. By focusing on platform gatekeepers rather than price effects, the DMA creates obligations around interoperability, data access, and default-setting that are more directly applicable to the AI consolidation problem. The gap between EU and US regulatory frameworks on this issue is significant, and for now it means that the world's largest AI market is governed by the weaker set of rules.

### The investigations already underway — and their limitations

The regulatory activity is real and worth tracking. The DOJ's Google search default case, currently awaiting a remedy ruling after a landmark liability finding in 2024, directly implicates the logic of AI default deals, since the AI agreement with Apple is structured analogously to the search deal that was found to violate antitrust law. The FTC has scrutinized the Microsoft–OpenAI investment structure, though it has not moved to block it. The UK's Competition and Markets Authority has published detailed AI market studies flagging many of the concentration risks described in this article.

The honest assessment of all this activity: it is slow, the remedies are uncertain, and the history of technology antitrust enforcement suggests that by the time any structural remedy is ordered, the default layer tends to be too entrenched to meaningfully restructure. Google's search remedy proceedings alone could take years beyond the initial ruling. The Microsoft–OpenAI story will be several product generations old before any regulatory intervention could realistically reshape it.

### The accountability gap — why antitrust alone won't fix this

Antitrust is the wrong primary frame for most readers. It is slow, technical, distant from daily life, and dependent on outcomes that will be shaped by judicial interpretations most people will never follow. That doesn't diminish its importance, it just makes it insufficient as the only response.

The more tractable frame is accountability: what do these companies actually owe users in terms of transparency, interoperability, and meaningful choice? That question doesn't require waiting for a court ruling. It can be asked by users, journalists, investors, and policymakers, and answering it creates the conditions under which better regulatory and market outcomes become possible. This is where the story stops being abstract and starts being personal.

## Beyond antitrust — the questions users and policymakers should be demanding answers to right now

If the consolidation has already happened and regulation is playing catch-up, what should the response actually look like? Three concrete accountability standards are reasonable to demand, achievable to implement, and long overdue.

### Transparency: who exactly is powering the AI answering your questions?

At minimum, users should know, in plain, disclosed language, which AI company's model is processing their queries on any given device or app. This is not currently required or consistently practiced. Many users have no idea whether their iPhone assistant is running on Apple's on-device model, Google's cloud infrastructure, or some combination of both.

A simple AI labeling standard, analogous to nutritional labeling on food or counterparty disclosure on financial products, would give users the foundational information needed to make any meaningful choice. It costs companies very little to implement. The argument against such transparency is essentially that companies prefer users not to notice who is powering their AI, a preference that should carry no weight in any serious policy conversation about user rights.

### Interoperability and portability: the right to take your AI context elsewhere

The second accountability standard is AI data portability, the right to take your interaction history, learned preferences, and contextual AI profile from one provider to another. Everything your AI assistant learns about how you work, what you prefer, and what contexts surround your queries is currently locked inside proprietary systems. Switching to an alternative AI means starting from zero, which is a meaningful switching cost even for users who are motivated to change.

This is the AI equivalent of number portability in telecommunications, the regulatory intervention that broke carrier lock-in by ensuring users could keep their phone number when changing providers. Your AI context should belong to you, not to the company that happened to be the default on the device you bought. The EU's GDPR data portability provisions and emerging AI Act obligations provide partial precedent here, though enforcement and standardization remain works in progress. 

### The opt-out question — meaningful choice vs. nominal choice

The third standard is the most fundamental: genuine opt-out rights. A meaningful opt-out from a default AI is not a buried setting twelve menus deep. It requires three things: visible disclosure that a default exists and what it means, an accessible settings path that doesn't require technical expertise, and at least one comparable alternative available on the device at setup.

This standard already exists, in concept, in the EU's Digital Markets Act requirements for browser and search default choice screens. Applying equivalent logic to AI defaults is a natural extension. Companies that object to this standard are implicitly acknowledging that their market position depends on users not knowing they have a choice, which is precisely the argument for requiring disclosure. Meaningful AI choice is not a radical demand. It is the baseline expectation of any functioning digital market.

## Frequently asked questions

**Q: Is it confirmed that Google is powering Apple Intelligence on iPhones?**
Reports from Bloomberg and other outlets have indicated that Apple and Google reached an agreement for Google's Gemini to power certain Apple Intelligence features, following the structure of their existing search default deal. Apple has confirmed that ChatGPT integration is part of Apple Intelligence and has signaled that other AI providers may be added. The full extent of Google's role in Apple Intelligence continues to develop as the feature set expands.

**Q: Can I actually switch my phone's default AI assistant to something else?**
On Android, you can change your default assistant in settings to alternatives like Claude or others that support the assistant API. On iOS, the integration of AI into system-level features is more tightly controlled by Apple. In practice, switching requires awareness that the option exists, some technical comfort with settings, and the trade-off of losing features that are deeply integrated with the operating system. For most users, the realistic answer is: technically yes, practically no.

**Q: Why isn't this getting more regulatory attention?**
It is getting some attention, the DOJ Google case, FTC scrutiny, and UK CMA studies all touch on these dynamics. The challenge is that AI default deals are structurally similar to search default deals, and regulators have spent years building a case on the search side. Applying that logic to AI requires either expanding existing cases or bringing new ones, both of which take time. Meanwhile, the defaults are already in place.

**Q: Does this mean smaller AI companies like Anthropic or Perplexity can't succeed?**
Not necessarily. They can, and do, build excellent products that attract users who actively seek them out. The concern is not about whether alternatives can exist, but whether they can compete at the distribution and data scale that default placement provides. A niche or premium AI product is a viable business. A challenger to Google and Microsoft at the default infrastructure layer faces structurally different obstacles.

**Q: What can I do as an individual user right now?**
Three things that matter: First, find out which AI is powering the assistant on your devices and whether you have a choice about it. Second, use your AI assistant less as a passive default and more as a deliberate tool, this at minimum makes you a more informed participant. Third, support policy advocacy and journalism that pushes for AI transparency and portability standards. Individual choices in a default-dominated market are limited; structural rules matter more.

## Conclusion: the window is still open — but not for long

The AI consolidation story has already played out at the infrastructure level. Two companies own the default AI layer across virtually every major device category. Most users are inside these ecosystems without awareness, without a clear opt-out, and without the transparency needed to make informed choices. Antitrust proceedings will eventually reach conclusions, but they will do so on a timeline that lags the technology by years.

The next time your phone answers a question, your email drafts a reply you didn't write, or your laptop summarizes a document in the background — notice it. Ask who built that. Ask whose data trained it. Ask whether you agreed to that arrangement, or whether it was simply set up without you.

One AI model. Two billion devices. That's not a feature announcement. 
That's infrastructure. And infrastructure this consequential 
deserves a public conversation before it becomes too 
entrenched to question.
