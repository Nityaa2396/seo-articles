# The EU AI Act Is Now Law. The Companies It Regulates Helped Write It.

This isn't an argument against AI regulation. 
Rules are overdue and this one is real. It's an argument 
for reading the fine print on who wrote them — and why that question 
matters more than any compliance announcement.

In August 2024, the **EU AI Act** officially entered into force as the world's first comprehensive legal framework governing artificial intelligence. EU officials called it a landmark. Consumer advocates called it long overdue. The major tech companies it was designed to constrain? Many of them called it something closer to expected, because in significant ways, they helped draft it.

That tension runs through everything that follows. The EU AI Act contains real rules with real consequences. Companies that ignore it face fines reaching into the hundreds of millions of euros. Some of its provisions on banned AI practices are genuinely strong. None of that is theater.

But the process that produced this law was never as neutral as the press releases suggested. Powerful corporate actors had access, resources, and sustained influence over the drafting process that civil society groups, academic researchers, and the public simply could not match. The result is a framework that reflects, at least in part, the preferences of the industry it was built to govern.

This article follows three threads: what the law actually requires in plain English, who shaped it and what they got for their effort, and how the **Brussels Effect** is quietly turning Europe's rules into everyone's rules.

## What the EU AI Act actually says (in plain English)

Before evaluating whether this law is a triumph of public interest governance or a sophisticated piece of incumbent protection, or, more likely, some uncomfortable combination of both, you need to understand what it actually says.

### The risk tier system — from banned to barely touched

The EU AI Act organizes all AI systems into four risk categories. Think of it like a building code: the higher the structure and the more people inside it, the stricter the inspection requirements. The more consequential the AI application, the more the law demands of the people deploying it.

At the top sits **Unacceptable Risk**: AI systems that are outright banned. This tier includes social scoring systems that rank citizens based on behavior, real-time remote biometric surveillance in public spaces (with carved-out law enforcement exceptions that we'll return to), and AI that exploits psychological vulnerabilities to manipulate behavior. These are not subject to compliance requirements, they're simply prohibited.

Below that is **High Risk**, and this is where the law has its sharpest operational teeth. High-risk AI systems include tools used in hiring and HR screening, credit scoring, medical device diagnostics, critical infrastructure management, and educational assessment. If an AI system makes or influences a consequential decision about a person's life, livelihood, or safety, there's a reasonable chance it lands here.

The third tier covers **Limited Risk**, systems with transparency obligations but not full compliance burdens. Chatbots must disclose they're AI. Deepfake content must be labeled. Users deserve to know when they're interacting with a machine. Meaningful, but comparatively lightweight. The bottom tier, **Minimal Risk**, covers the vast majority of AI, spam filters, recommendation algorithms, inventory tools, and is essentially unregulated under this framework.

### What high-risk actually demands of companies

Landing in the high-risk tier is not a checkbox exercise. **EU AI Act compliance** for high-risk systems requires mandatory risk assessments before deployment, robust human oversight mechanisms, data governance and quality controls, detailed technical documentation, and registration in an EU-maintained public database. These obligations persist throughout the system's entire operational life, not just at launch.

The compliance burden falls not just on the AI companies building these tools but on the organizations deploying them. A hospital using an AI diagnostic system, a bank deploying an automated credit model, an HR platform using algorithmic candidate screening, all of them are in scope. For many organizations outside the technology sector, this is a genuinely new operational responsibility.

The costs are real and the competitive implications are uneven. Larger organizations with dedicated legal, compliance, and technical teams are better positioned to absorb this burden than smaller ones. That's a structural feature of how this law was designed, not an incidental side effect.

## The timeline and the teeth — what enforcement actually looks like

Understanding what the EU AI Act requires is one thing. Understanding how and when those requirements will actually be enforced, and by whom, is a different question, and arguably the more important one.

### The staggered rollout schedule

The Act doesn't flip on all at once. Implementation is phased across several years, which creates preparation time and, critics argue, ample opportunity for strategic delay.

The Act entered into force in **August 2024**. Provisions banning prohibited practices took effect in **February 2025**. Governance structures and GPAI rules apply from **August 2025**. The full high-risk AI requirements become enforceable in **August 2026**. Certain legacy systems already deployed before the Act passed get a grace period extending to **2027**.

Proponents of the staggered approach argue it reflects regulatory realism, organizations need time to build compliance infrastructure, and a hard cutover would create chaos. Critics counter that phased implementation is also a political gift to the regulated, giving well-resourced incumbents years to shape enforcement norms before they kick in. The timeline serves both purposes simultaneously.

### The fine structure — large numbers, layered caveats

The headline **AI Act fines** are designed to get attention, and they succeed. Violations of prohibited practice provisions carry penalties of up to €35 million or **7% of global annual turnover**, whichever is higher. Other violations carry up to €15 million or 3%. Providing incorrect information to regulators can cost up to €7.5 million or 1.5%.

For a company with $300 billion in annual revenue, a 3% fine represents $9 billion, genuinely painful even for a hyperscaler. For an early-stage startup, a fraction of that could be existential. The fine structure operates very differently depending on where you sit in the market.

These are **maximum** penalties, not mandatory ones. Regulators have broad discretion, and enforcement under **GDPR** offers a cautionary precedent. Despite that law's €20 million or 4% of turnover maximums, the majority of fines issued over six years have been modest, and the largest fines against the most powerful companies took years of legal proceedings to materialize.

## Who wrote this law — follow the lobbyists

The documented record of corporate lobbying during the EU AI Act's development is not a fringe concern, it's a matter of public record, and it raises legitimate questions about whose interests the final text was written to serve.

### The access problem — who was in the room

Research from the **Corporate Europe Observatory**, a nonprofit that tracks lobbying influence in EU policymaking, documented that Big Tech companies and their affiliated trade associations accounted for a disproportionate share of documented meetings with EU officials during the Act's development. 

**Google, Microsoft, IBM, and OpenAI** all had significant formal and informal engagement with the legislative process. This is documented through the EU's own transparency registers, which log meetings between lobbyists and EU officials, though the registers are widely acknowledged to capture only a portion of actual influence activity.

Academic researchers studying algorithmic harm, civil liberties organizations tracking biometric surveillance, labor advocates focused on algorithmic management, and consumer protection groups all participated, but with a fraction of the access, resources, and sustained presence that corporate actors deployed. In AI regulation, where technical complexity itself creates barriers to participation, that imbalance was particularly sharp.

### What they pushed for — and what they got

**Definitional narrowing** was a consistent industry priority: push to define "high-risk" tightly enough that more systems fall outside it. Analysis of successive drafts shows the high-risk category was progressively narrowed through the negotiating process, with many AI applications that advocacy groups wanted included landing in lower tiers instead. **Regulatory capture** doesn't require explicit corruption, it just requires that one side of the negotiation has more lawyers, more technical experts, and more time.

The clearest civil society loss was on **biometric surveillance**. Despite sustained opposition from digital rights organizations across Europe, the final text includes significant law enforcement carve-outs allowing real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces under defined circumstances. Those circumstances, critics argue, are defined broadly enough to render the prohibition largely symbolic for state actors. 

### Why regulatory capture is a structural problem, not a scandal

**Regulatory capture** of the kind observable in the EU AI Act's development isn't primarily a story about corrupt officials or illegal influence. It's a story about information asymmetry and institutional incentives that play out predictably wherever complex technical industries meet democratic governance.

Regulators cannot understand what they're regulating without help from the regulated. AI systems are extraordinarily technical, rapidly evolving, and genuinely difficult to assess from the outside. The EU Commission and Parliament needed expertise they didn't have internally, and the primary available source of that expertise was the industry itself. That dependency is both rational and structurally dangerous.

A fair reading of the record shows EU negotiators did push back on industry in meaningful ways. Some prohibited practices provisions are stronger than industry wanted. The GPAI chapter exists at all because lawmakers overrode significant corporate resistance. The Act's fundamental architecture, a risk-based framework with real enforcement teeth, survived industry pressure to replace it with voluntary codes of conduct. These are genuine accomplishments, not cosmetic ones.

But the structural problem remains. When the regulated help design the rules, the rules tend to favor the regulated. That's one of the most replicated findings in political science and regulatory economics, with evidence spanning banking, pharmaceuticals, and telecommunications. The relevant question for anyone evaluating compliance claims isn't just "has this company met the requirements?" It's "who designed those requirements, and what were they protecting?"

## The Brussels Effect — how Europe's rules become the world's rules

Even if you live outside the European Union, even if your company has no European customers, the EU AI Act may already be shaping the AI tools you use. That's the **Brussels Effect** at work.

### What the Brussels Effect is and why it's real

Columbia Law professor **Anu Bradford** coined the term "Brussels Effect" to describe a specific regulatory phenomenon: EU standards tend to become global standards, not through treaty obligations or political pressure, but through the simple economics of multinational business. 

The mechanism is straightforward. Companies selling into the EU market must meet EU standards. When those standards are stringent enough, it becomes more cost-effective to build one product to EU specifications than to maintain separate versions for different regulatory environments. The high-compliance version becomes the default, and European rules travel without European enforcement.

The clearest proof of concept is **GDPR**. Enacted in 2018, it became a de facto global privacy standard within years. California's **CCPA** drew heavily on GDPR frameworks. Brazil's **LGPD** was modeled on it. Dozens of countries across Asia, Africa, and Latin America adopted GDPR-adjacent frameworks, not because the EU compelled them to, but because the market logic made convergence rational. **Brussels Effect AI regulation** follows the same logic applied to a more consequential technology.

It's worth being clear-eyed about what this means. The Brussels Effect amplifies whatever standards Europe adopts, good or flawed. If the AI Act's provisions are well-designed, the world benefits from their spread. If those provisions were shaped to protect incumbents and limit competition, those dynamics scale globally too.


## Five questions to ask before trusting any AI compliance claim

The EU AI Act creates an enormous market for compliance theater. Companies will publish compliance reports, hire AI ethics officers, and issue press releases announcing their commitment to the Act's principles. Some of those claims will reflect genuine institutional commitment. Others will be sophisticated performance.

**1. Who funded the lobbying that shaped these rules?** If a company is now claiming compliance credit for a framework their own lobbyists helped design, ask what provisions were softened, narrowed, or eliminated during that process. Compliance with self-authored rules is a limited form of accountability.

**2. Which tier did they land in, and how?** If a company's AI products consistently fall just outside the high-risk category, ask whether that reflects genuine low-risk applications or definitional maneuvering. The high-risk classification was contested during drafting for a reason.

**3. What does their human oversight actually look like?** High-risk AI systems require human oversight mechanisms, but "human in the loop" can mean meaningful review or a checkbox that nobody reads. Ask for specifics about how oversight decisions are made, documented, and audited.

**4. Which national regulator will enforce their compliance?** If a company is headquartered in an EU member state with a known history of light-touch enforcement and an economic interest in attracting AI investment, the name of their compliance framework matters less than who's checking it.

**5. What happens when the system makes a wrong call?** Real compliance includes meaningful redress mechanisms, ways for people harmed by AI decisions to understand what happened and seek correction. If a company can't clearly explain that process, the compliance architecture may be more presentable than it is functional.

## Conclusion: The law is real. So is the question behind it.

The EU AI Act is a genuine regulatory achievement. It establishes real obligations, real penalties, and a real institutional infrastructure for AI governance in the world's second-largest economy. After years of voluntary principles and toothless codes of conduct, binding law represents meaningful progress.

But laws are never neutral documents. They're products of political processes, and political processes reflect power. The evidence that major technology companies shaped the EU AI Act's provisions in ways that favor incumbents, narrow enforcement scope, and raise barriers for smaller competitors is not speculative, it's documented, traceable, and consistent with well-established patterns of how complex technical industries interact with democratic governance.

None of this means the law is worthless or that compliance is meaningless. It means that compliance claims deserve scrutiny, enforcement choices deserve public attention, and the companies now celebrating their readiness for the AI Act deserve questions about the role they played in writing it.

The Brussels Effect ensures these rules will travel. Whether they travel in a form that genuinely protects people or primarily protects the companies that drafted them is not a question answered once at enactment. It gets answered through enforcement decisions, legal challenges, and the sustained attention of journalists, advocates, and citizens willing to keep pressing on it.

---

The next time a company announces its EU AI Act compliance, ask them one question: which provisions did you lobby to soften before you signed off on the rest?

That question will tell you more than any press release.
