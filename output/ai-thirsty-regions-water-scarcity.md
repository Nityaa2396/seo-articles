# AI Is Thirsty. The Places Paying the Price Can't Afford It.

*In Abilene, Texas, residents are on drought restrictions — limiting showers, skipping lawn watering, watching the sky for rain that doesn't come. Down the road, a data center the size of Central Park is being built to power ChatGPT. It will consume enough electricity to light 750,000 homes. And it will need water — a lot of it — to keep from melting down.*

*This is not a hypothetical future. It's already happening.*

*This isn't a case against building AI infrastructure. It's a case for building it with transparency, planning, and community input.*

*And Abilene is just one city in a growing list of places that didn't know they were in a fight over water until Big Tech showed up.*

---

## The Location Problem Nobody Planned For

When companies decide where to build data centers, they think about three things: cheap land, cheap power, and friendly regulations. Water is barely an afterthought.

That calculation is now colliding with a crisis nobody in the industry prepared for.

According to analysis by the World Resources Institute, roughly **40% of existing and planned US data centers are located in regions already classified as facing "high" or "extremely high" water stress**. That number climbs to 43% for the largest facilities — the hyperscale campuses consuming more than 40 megawatt-hours of electricity per hour. Just five states — Virginia, Texas, Arizona, California, and Illinois — account for 72% of these new centers. Every one of them has documented water challenges.

The IEA estimates that an average 100-megawatt data center consumes around **2 million liters of water every single day**. Globally, the sector already consumes more than 560 billion liters annually, with projections reaching 1,200 billion liters by 2030. That water doesn't come from nowhere. It comes from the same aquifers, reservoirs, and municipal systems that communities, farms, and ecosystems depend on.

This is the story of where that water is running out — and who's fighting back.

---

## Texas: The Biggest Conflict in the Biggest State

Texas is ground zero for the data center water crisis in America.

The state has 391 data centers — second only to Virginia — and that number is growing fast. Texas offers what Big Tech loves: massive land, deregulated energy markets, no state income tax, and local governments that roll out the welcome mat. What it increasingly cannot offer is water.

OpenAI, Oracle, and SoftBank's **Stargate project** — a $500 billion AI infrastructure initiative — chose Abilene, Texas as its flagship site. The campus will span over 1,000 acres when complete, running hundreds of thousands of specialized AI chips. The surrounding county is already under drought conditions. Residents face water conservation mandates. Farmers and ranchers in the Panhandle are operating under water restrictions that have been in place for years.

Microsoft's Abilene data center has already consumed **463 million gallons of water in its first two years of operation**. The broader Texas projection, from a study by the Houston Advanced Research Center and University of Houston, is staggering: data centers in Texas will use **49 billion gallons of water in 2025** — climbing to **399 billion gallons by 2030**. To put that in perspective, 399 billion gallons is enough to drain Lake Mead — the largest reservoir in the US — by more than 16 feet in a single year.

In Amarillo, residents showed up in force at city council meetings to oppose selling municipal water to a planned data center in nearby Carson County. In August 2025, the Tucson, Arizona city council unanimously rejected an Amazon-linked data center — already approved for a land purchase — after the council refused to allow it to connect to the city's water system. The company's facility would have immediately become Tucson Water's single largest consumer.

The Texas state government, however, has no regulations on data center water use. While residents are told to conserve, corporations operate without limits. The inequality is not subtle, and it is not going unnoticed.

---

## Northern Virginia: The World's Data Center Capital Runs on Secrecy

Northern Virginia is unlike anywhere else on earth. Roughly two-thirds of the world's internet traffic passes through a handful of counties in this region. There are approximately 300 data centers already operating there, with dozens more planned. It is the densest concentration of data center infrastructure on the planet.

It is also where a quiet war over water is being fought — largely in secret.

When residents in Botetourt County tried to find out how much water Google's planned data center would use, the Western Virginia Water Authority blocked the information. The contracts were shielded by non-disclosure agreements. A local news outlet, the Roanoke Rambler, took the water authority to court just to access the numbers its own government had agreed to.

The pattern is consistent across the region. Data center developers negotiate water agreements with local utilities, NDA clauses prevent public disclosure, and communities are left unable to assess the cumulative impact on their water supply. By the time residents organize, the permits are already approved and construction is often underway.

Beyond secrecy, there is the land question. Dozens of new data centers in Virginia are pushing into rural counties, consuming farmland that has fed communities for generations. County residents watch fields get replaced with gray concrete buildings surrounded by security fencing and cooling towers — and then discover the water agreements only after a local reporter files a lawsuit.

## Chile: A Drought That's Already 15 Years Old

Chile is experiencing one of the most severe megadroughts on record — a dry spell that began in 2010 and shows no signs of breaking. The government has enforced water rationing in the capital, Santiago. Farmers in the Atacama region have watched irrigation supplies collapse. The Santiago Aquifer, which provides water to millions of residents, is under sustained pressure from climate change and overextraction.

Into this water emergency, Google proposed a $200 million data center in Santiago.

A Chilean environmental court partially reversed Google's authorization in early 2024, ordering the company to account for the effects of climate change on the local aquifer and reconsider its cooling system. Community protests had already put the project under scrutiny. In September 2024, **Google announced it was pausing the project entirely and starting its permit process over from scratch** — a significant reversal for a company that first obtained permits in 2020.

The reason this story matters beyond Chile is that Google had actually done something technically legal: it purchased existing water rights from their current holders, acquiring the right to draw from the Santiago Aquifer in a different municipality. Under Chile's water privatization laws — a remnant of the Pinochet era — water rights can be bought and sold independently of land. Google wasn't breaking rules. It was following them. And a court still said that wasn't good enough.

Chile is currently facing a drought expected to last until **2040**. The data center market there is simultaneously projected to grow at 8.27% annually through 2030. That is the contradiction at the center of this crisis: the places most attractive for data center development — stable governments, renewable energy, strategic geography — are often the same places where water is already running out.

## Saudi Arabia, UAE, and the Desert Paradox

If Chile is a cautionary tale, the Gulf states are something closer to a gamble.

Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates are aggressively courting data center investment as part of their national AI strategies. Both countries are home to some of the most water-scarce environments on the planet — desert nations where virtually all freshwater comes from energy-intensive desalination, and where temperatures routinely push cooling systems to their limits.

The logic, at least from a business perspective, has a certain internal consistency: these countries have cheap, abundant energy (still largely fossil-fuel derived), favorable investment regulations, and strategic ambitions to become AI hubs. The environmental contradiction — building water-hungry cooling infrastructure in places that have almost no water — is treated as an engineering problem to be solved rather than a constraint to be respected.

Cooling towers that evaporate water don't work well in these climates anyway; the heat and aridity accelerate evaporation, increasing water loss. Many facilities are shifting toward air cooling, which is less efficient but doesn't require water. The tradeoff is higher electricity consumption — which in the Gulf means more fossil fuels burned to cool AI servers processing queries from the other side of the world.

---

## India and China: Scale Makes Everything Worse

In terms of sheer scale, India and China represent the sharpest end of this problem.

Both countries have massive and growing data center footprints. Both have significantly higher proportions of data centers located in water-stressed regions compared to the US. Both are experiencing climate-driven water stress across their most populated and economically active zones.

In India, data center clusters have grown rapidly around Mumbai, Hyderabad, Chennai, and Pune — cities already facing severe groundwater depletion and seasonal water scarcity. The rapid build-out of AI infrastructure is layering new industrial demand onto urban water systems that are already strained by population growth and erratic monsoons.

China's data center expansion is concentrated in coastal and inland manufacturing corridors where water stress from agriculture, industry, and urbanization is already acute. The government has pushed data centers toward cooler northern regions to reduce cooling costs — but those regions often face their own water constraints and rely on coal for electricity.

The data transparency problem is even more acute in these markets. Reporting on data center water use is sparse, voluntary disclosure is uncommon, and independent research faces access barriers. The scale of the problem is likely understated relative to what the numbers would actually show if full reporting were required.

## What the Communities Are Doing About It

The backlash is real, and it's getting organized.

In Chile, street protests led to Google's permit being suspended. In Uruguay, similar community pressure shaped how Google's data center there was ultimately designed. In the Netherlands, local governments have imposed moratoriums on new data center construction near Amsterdam, citing water and energy strain. In Texas, rural communities are showing up at county commission meetings in numbers that local officials haven't seen in years.

In Northern Virginia, journalists and public interest lawyers are using public records laws to pry open the NDA-shielded water agreements that local governments have signed with tech companies.

What unites these fights is the same core grievance: communities were not consulted. Decisions about how their water would be used — water they depend on for drinking, farming, and economic survival — were made by distant companies and approved by local officials who prioritized tax revenue and job announcements over long-term resource protection.

Some companies are responding. AWS has moved several facilities to 100% recycled water. Google has signed watershed partnership agreements in Chile, California, Taiwan, and France to support water replenishment in the regions where it operates. Microsoft has pledged to be "water positive" by 2030 — meaning it will return more water to watersheds than it consumes.

Critics point out that these pledges don't help the communities being impacted today. A watershed replenishment project in one county doesn't fill an aquifer in another. And as long as data center operators can locate in water-stressed areas without regulatory limits on consumption, the incentive to conserve is voluntary — and profit will generally win.

---

## The Transparency Gap Is the Real Problem

One statistic sits at the center of all of this: **fewer than one-third of data center operators track their own water consumption**.

The industry is extracting billions of gallons from aquifers and municipal systems that communities depend on, and most of it isn't even being measured. What isn't measured can't be regulated. What isn't regulated isn't constrained. And what isn't constrained grows until someone protests, or a court intervenes, or a city runs dry.

Water stress is already affecting one in four data centers globally — a number that will grow as climate change tightens supply in the regions where this infrastructure has been built. By 2050, MSCI analysis suggests that a significant portion of existing facilities will experience regular periods where local water demand exceeds supply by more than 60%.

The data center industry is building for 2030. It is locating infrastructure based on 2024 conditions. The climate models are already showing what 2050 looks like in Texas, in northern Chile, in the Gulf, in Mumbai. That mismatch — between where data centers are going and where water will still be — is a planning gap we still have time to close. But only if we start measuring it.

---

## What Needs to Happen

The path forward requires three things that the industry has resisted.

**Mandatory disclosure.** Companies should be required to report water consumption at the facility level — not as aggregate corporate sustainability figures, but as specific numbers tied to specific locations. Communities deserve to know what agreements their governments are signing.

**Location standards.** Permitting for large data centers in high water-stress regions should require environmental impact assessments that account for climate projections through 2050, not just current conditions. If a region is on track for severe water scarcity within 30 years, that data center's operational lifetime, that needs to be part of the approval process.

**Community standing.** Residents in affected areas need legal mechanisms to participate in permit decisions that affect their water supplies. NDAs that shield water usage agreements from public scrutiny are not compatible with democratic accountability over shared resources.

The AI boom is real. The infrastructure it requires is real. And the water those data centers need is also real — finite, unevenly distributed, and already running short in the places being chosen to host the world's AI future.

The people of Abilene didn't vote to become the water supply for ChatGPT. Neither did the people of Santiago, or the farmers of the Texas Panhandle, or the residents of Botetourt County who just wanted to know what their water authority had agreed to.

They deserve to know. And they deserve to have a say before the aquifer runs dry.
