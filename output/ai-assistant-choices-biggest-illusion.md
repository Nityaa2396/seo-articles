# The Biggest Illusion in AI Right Now Is That You Have Choices.

This isn't an argument against using AI assistants. 
It's an argument for understanding what you're actually 
choosing — and why the menu is shorter than it looks.

---

## Introduction

Imagine you've spent a week frustrated with Microsoft Copilot. It keeps missing context, the responses feel generic, and you're convinced a switch to Google Gemini will finally fix it. So you migrate. You spend an afternoon updating workflows, resetting preferences, and mentally committing to the new product. Then you open Gemini and get a response that feels, uncannily, like the one you just left. Same fluency. Same limitations. Same vague sense that you're talking to something very capable of sounding helpful without quite being it.

That's not a coincidence. Any honest AI assistant comparison in 2025 has to start there.

The market presents four major players, Google Gemini, Microsoft Copilot, Apple Siri, and Amazon Alexa, as genuine competitors slugging it out for your attention and trust. Different branding, different pricing tiers, different marketing budgets. But what if the competition is mostly aesthetic? What if what looks like a contested market is structurally closer to an oligopoly wearing four different outfits?

This article won't rank these assistants by feature count or benchmark score. It will interrogate whether those features represent real differences at all. A sharper mental model for evaluating AI assistants is waiting on the other side, along with a more skeptical eye for the marketing claims that will keep coming.

## 1. The AI assistant market looks competitive — here's why that's misleading

### 1.1 The illusion of a crowded marketplace

In the past three years, the AI assistant space has seen a relentless parade of launches, rebrandings, and "major updates." Google retired Bard and relaunched it as Gemini with a splashy campaign. Microsoft quietly buried Cortana and pivoted hard to Copilot, embedding it across the entire Office 365 ecosystem. These aren't just naming decisions, they're narrative resets, designed to create the impression of reinvention. A new name on an updated product is not the same thing as a new product.

Launch frequency gets mistaken for innovation constantly. When a company pushes a major update every few months, it signals investment in marketing and competitive positioning, not necessarily that the underlying architecture has meaningfully changed, or that the gap between products has widened rather than narrowed.

The AI assistant competition narrative is largely manufactured through press cycles. The more important question rarely surfaces: are these products actually getting more different from each other, or more similar?

### 1.2 Feature parity as a red flag, not a benchmark

Here's a telling data point: in 2025, nearly every major AI assistant supports multimodal input, code generation, document summarization, tone adjustment, and real-time web search. If you built a feature checklist and ran each product through it, the columns would look almost identical. Most comparison articles interpret this as "healthy competition driving rapid improvement." That reading is backwards.

When competing products converge on nearly identical features, that's not innovation racing toward the top. It's a symptom of shared upstream infrastructure, the same foundational technologies, the same training paradigms, the same capability ceiling. Think of store-brand products manufactured in the same factory but sold under different labels. The packaging differs. The product, largely, does not.

Feature parity should make you more suspicious, not more confident. It's a sign of market convergence, not a measure of consumer benefit.

### 1.3 What "competition" in AI would actually have to mean

Genuine competition in AI assistants would require architectural independence (systems trained with different approaches to reasoning), distinct training data philosophies (different sources, different consent frameworks, different curation choices), and meaningfully different performance in real-world use, not just different wake words.

By that standard, most of what gets called competition today is product differentiation, not market competition. These are different claims. One is about branding and interface. The other is about whether you actually have a real choice. That distinction runs through everything that follows.

## 2. Who actually built what you're using — the foundation model problem

### 2.1 What a foundation model is (and why it's the only layer that matters)

A foundation model is the large-scale pre-trained system that forms the cognitive core of an AI assistant, the thing that actually determines how the assistant reasons, generates language, and understands context. Everything you see as a user, the interface, the personality settings, the integrations with your calendar or inbox, sits on top of the foundation model like furniture in a rented apartment.

The furniture can be moved around. The walls are fixed. When two products share the same foundation model or closely related model lineage, their differences are furniture-level, not structural. This is not a minor technical caveat. It's the central fact that most AI assistant comparison articles skip entirely.

Consumers and businesses routinely make switching decisions and long-term platform commitments based on product-layer differences without ever asking what's underneath. That's the gap this article is trying to close.

### 2.2 The licensing and partnership web beneath the brand names

Here's the web that doesn't get discussed in product reviews. Microsoft Copilot runs on OpenAI's GPT models, a relationship formalized through Microsoft's multi-billion dollar investment in OpenAI. Amazon has moved its Alexa platform toward a more capable large language model architecture, with reports of external model infrastructure informing its "Alexa Plus" evolution. Dozens of enterprise AI products marketed under distinct brand names are simply Claude, GPT-4, or Gemini accessed through an API with a custom interface on top.

Asking "who made the underlying model?" is the most important question a consumer or B2B buyer can ask before committing to a platform. It almost never appears on the checkout screen, the product landing page, or in the vendor's pitch deck. That omission is not accidental, it would collapse the illusion of meaningful differentiation.

The foundation model ecosystem is actually quite concentrated: a handful of organizations, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, and Meta, produce the models that power most of what's marketed to you as competing products.

## 3. Big tech ecosystems are doing the real work of lock-in

### 3.1 Your device already chose for you

The AI assistant "choice" most people make wasn't really a choice. If you're on an iPhone, Siri is your default AI layer, embedded in your device's operating system, your AirPods, your HomePod, and your Mac. If you're running Windows 11, Copilot is baked into the taskbar and keyboard shortcut before you've opened a browser. If you're on Android, Gemini has gradually replaced Google Assistant as the default.

Ecosystem entrenchment means that for most users, the comparison moment never actually arrives. The decision was pre-made by the hardware purchase, often years earlier, for completely unrelated reasons. The AI assistant market competition you're reading about is, for most people, largely academic.

This matters because the real competitive arena isn't between AI assistants, it's between device ecosystems. That's a consolidation story that predates AI entirely.

### 3.2 Subscription bundling and the Microsoft 365 / Google One effect

If hardware lock-in doesn't catch you, subscription bundling will. Microsoft 365 now integrates Copilot at multiple tiers, meaning enterprise customers who switch AI assistants are effectively asking their organizations to evaluate an entirely different productivity suite. Google One and Google Workspace create an equivalent gravitational pull on the Gemini side.

The switching cost is rarely measured in dollars. It's measured in workflow disruption, retraining time, data portability friction, and the organizational inertia of getting a team to move from one set of embedded tools to another. Big tech AI ecosystem lock-in functions exactly as designed: not by making the product irreplaceable, but by making replacement expensive in ways that don't show up on a price comparison page.

Many organizations that believe they chose an AI assistant actually had the choice made for them by a vendor relationship they established years ago.

## 4. The best AI assistant 2025 — what the rankings miss

### 4.1 Why benchmark scores flatter the wrong things

Every few months, a new set of benchmark results circulates, and the discourse around the best AI assistant in 2025 gets temporarily reset by whichever product topped the latest leaderboard. These benchmarks typically measure narrow, reproducible tasks: trivia accuracy, coding completion on standardized problems, summarization speed on clean text. They are engineered to produce a winner, and vendors engineer their products to perform well on them.

The problem is that these benchmarks don't reflect how most people actually use AI assistants: ambiguous requests, multi-step reasoning, context retention across long conversations, handling sensitive or nuanced topics without going off the rails. A product can consistently top benchmark rankings while delivering a nearly identical real-world experience to its competitors, because the benchmark measures a sliver of what actually matters.

Leaderboard performance is a marketing tool dressed as an evaluation tool. Treat it accordingly.

### 4.2 The questions no review article is asking

A structurally honest AI assistant evaluation would ask questions that almost no mainstream review article currently does. Who trained the underlying model, and what was the training data? Were the creators of that data notified or compensated? Does this assistant's personalization data stay on your device, sit in a corporate cloud, or get used to retrain future model versions? What happens to your data if you cancel your subscription?

These are not niche privacy concerns for the technically paranoid. They are baseline accountability questions for any service that processes your professional communications, personal questions, and intellectual work. The fact that they rarely appear in comparison articles isn't because the answers are unimportant, it's because the answers would make uncomfortable reading for the companies whose advertising those publications depend on.

### 4.3 A more honest evaluation matrix

Instead of a feature checklist, here's a framework worth applying to any AI assistant before you commit. Model provenance transparency: can the company clearly state what model powers the product and where the training data came from? Data governance clarity: what are your rights over your interaction data? Portability of personal context: can you export your preferences, history, and customizations if you leave? Genuine architectural differentiation: is there meaningful evidence this product performs differently because it was built differently, or just branded differently?

The current market satisfies almost none of these standards consistently. That's not an indictment of specific products, it's an indictment of an industry that has not yet been forced to meet them. Knowing the standard exists is how you start demanding it.

## Conclusion

The AI assistant market in 2025 is sophisticated, well-funded, and impressively polished. It is not, in any structurally meaningful sense, a competitive market delivering genuine choice. Beneath four distinct brand identities, there are a handful of foundation models, a web of investment and licensing relationships, and an industry-wide incentive to manufacture the appearance of differentiation rather than the substance of it.

The products are useful. But usefulness and genuine competition are different claims, and conflating them is exactly what the marketing is designed to make you do.

The sharper mental model: treat the AI assistant landscape as a converging oligopoly with a competitive surface and a consolidated core. Evaluate products based on ecosystem fit, use-case performance, and the structural questions, model provenance, data governance, portability, that reviews rarely ask. Demand transparency from vendors as a condition of trust, not as a bonus feature. And stay genuinely skeptical of any announcement, ranking, or comparison article, including this one, that doesn't show its work at the foundation level.

**The next time you read an AI assistant comparison, ask the one question those articles almost never answer: who actually built the engine? Everything else is just the paint job.**
